# MyTournament.PB × ThePickleBase
# Coffee Meeting Prep Script
# For: Mike (Admin PB) meeting Grace (ThePickleBase)
# Read this the night before and morning of. Don't memorise — absorb the intent.

---

## MINDSET BEFORE YOU WALK IN

You are not going in to ask for anything.
You are going in to understand what they're building and share what you're building.
If something makes sense, it will surface naturally.
You have something they need. They have something you need. Neither side is doing the other a favour.

You are 3 weeks old. That is not a weakness — it means you have no bad habits yet, no legacy infrastructure, and you built something real fast enough that an 18.5K account found you and reached out first.

Walk in curious, not hungry.

---

## PART 1 — HOW TO INTRODUCE YOURSELF

### The 60-second version (if she asks "so tell me about yourself")

> "My background is actually in corporate — I spent about three years in procurement at SLB managing international suppliers across APAC and Europe, and before that I was a financial consultant at OCBC. So I know the business side reasonably well.
>
> In 2024 I left to go full-time into Web3 — research, trading, on-chain stuff. That chapter's winding down and I'm figuring out what's next. In the meantime I got seriously into pickleball.
>
> I kept missing tournaments because the info was scattered across Sportssync, Baseline, Sports We Play — three different platforms, no single place to check. So I just built one. Zero dev background, but I've been learning with AI tools — vibe coding basically. mytournamentpb.com launched in May. It's a live tournament calendar: every tournament, every deadline, every prize pool, colour-coded by platform. The tracker already covers 60+ tournaments and over RM1 million in visible prize money.
>
> Instagram started three weeks ago. 112 followers. I go by Admin PB — the brand is faceless, like a group-chat admin who does the homework so players don't have to."

**Then stop. Let her respond. Don't keep talking.**

---

### If she asks "why pickleball specifically?"

> "I play. I understand the problem from the inside. And honestly — nobody else was doing this properly for Malaysia. The association has a calendar but it's not mobile-friendly, not social, and doesn't tell you what it costs to enter or when registration closes. Players were just missing tournaments. I thought — this is a solvable problem."

---

### If she asks "is this your full-time thing?"

> "Side project for now. I have about 3 to 5 hours a week for it. But the infrastructure I've built — the tracker, the website, the weekly drops — is designed to run lean. Data sweep twice a week, publish Friday, done. The goal is to make it sustainable at this pace first, then scale."

---

### If she asks "how did you build the website with no dev background?"

> "AI tools — mainly Claude. I describe what I want in plain English, it writes the code, I test it, I iterate. It took a few weeks of back and forth but the site is fully functional. Live data from a Google Sheet, filters by state and skill level, calendar view, search. I basically act as the product manager and the AI is the engineer."

## PART 2 — WHAT YOU'RE LOOKING FOR

### Don't lead with this. Let her ask first.

If she asks "what kind of collaboration did you have in mind?" — answer honestly and specifically:

> "Honestly I came in wanting to understand what you're building first. But the angle that makes obvious sense to me is this — you cover what happened, we cover what's coming. You write the recap on Alliance Bank Malaysia Open, we list where to register for the next one. Same audience, different jobs. No overlap, clean handoff."

**Then ask:** *"Does that match where your head was at?"*

---

### If she pushes for more specifics:

> "Three things I think could work:
>
> One — editorial to registration pipeline. At the bottom of your Malaysia tournament coverage, a block that says 'want to compete? here are open registrations this month' linking to mytournamentpb.com. You already have the readers. We give them somewhere to go next.
>
> Two — data sharing. Our tracker is detailed — prize pool, entry fee, deadline, state, which platform to register on. If that's useful for what you're building on the calendar side, let's talk about how it could work.
>
> Three — cross-audience. You have 18.5K. We're three weeks old. One shoutout post from you is worth months of organic growth for us. In return — first access to our tracker data, co-branded tournament drops, whatever makes sense for your audience."

## PART 3 — QUESTIONS TO ASK HER

Ask these naturally across the conversation. Not all at once. Pick 3 or 4 that feel relevant.

**About their product:**
- "Your events calendar — is that something you're actively building out, or more of a side feature right now?"
- "The Malaysia section on your site — is that a focus area or more organic?"
- "You cover Southeast Asia broadly — how much of your audience is specifically Malaysian?"
- "Courts is listed as coming soon — what's the plan there?"

**About their team:**
- "How big is the team behind ThePickleBase?"
- "Is it full-time for you or more of a passion project as well?"

**About their business model (ask this later when it feels natural):**
- "How are you monetising right now — brand partnerships, affiliate, something else?"

**About the collaboration specifically:**
- "What made you reach out to us specifically? What did you see that made you think there's something here?"
- "What would a good outcome from today look like for you?"

## PART 4 — ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS THEY WILL LIKELY ASK YOU

### "How many followers do you have?"

Don't be defensive. Be direct and pivot to the real metric.

> "112 on Instagram right now — three weeks old so still early. But the metric I care more about is saves. Our tournament list slides get saved at a higher rate than they get liked, which means people are actually using the information, not just scrolling past it. That's the signal I'm watching."

---

### "How do you monetise?"

> "Not yet. The plan is featured listings once traffic is consistent — organisers pay to be highlighted in the weekly drop and on the site. Paddle affiliates second. But right now it's purely about building the most reliable tournament calendar in Malaysia. Monetisation follows trust."

---

### "Why faceless? Why not build a personal brand?"

> "Deliberate choice. Admin PB works because it's not about me — it's about the information. Players share it because it's useful, not because they know who made it. And it means the brand doesn't depend on my face or my following. It can scale without me being the product."

---

### "Are you worried about platforms like Sportssync building this themselves?"

> "Not really. Sportssync, Baseline, SWP — they're each trying to get organisers onto their platform. They have a structural reason to not aggregate across competitors. We have zero reason to take sides. We list everything, from everywhere. That's our advantage — we're the only neutral party."

---

### "What's your timeline for the website?"

> "It's already live — mytournamentpb.com. Full tournament calendar, filters by state and skill level, calendar view, live search, updates automatically from the tracker. Launched a couple of weeks ago."

---

### "How do you keep the data accurate?"

> "Two sweeps a week — Monday and Thursday. I check Sportssync, Baseline, and Sports We Play manually. Anything new goes into the tracker. Deadlines that pass get marked closed. The Friday post is built from the tracker, so if the tracker is clean, the post is clean. Not glamorous but it works."

---

### "Would you ever expand beyond Malaysia?"

> "Eventually, maybe. But not until Malaysia is done properly. The problem I'm solving is real and specific — Malaysian players missing Malaysian tournaments. If I solve that well, the model probably works in other SEA markets. But not before the home market is right."

---

### "Are you open to investment or bringing on partners?"

> "Not at this stage. Too early. Right now I want to prove the concept fully before any of that conversation makes sense. Ask me again in 6 months."

---

### "How do you see us working together long-term?"

> "Honestly — you're the media layer, we're the data layer. You tell the stories, we run the registrations. If Malaysian pickleball grows, both of those jobs get bigger. I don't think we need a formal agreement to start. We can do something small, see how it lands for both audiences, and build from there."

---

### If they ask you to help fix or build their calendar website

> "I can take a look at it — I build things with AI so I can usually figure out what's going on. But I want to be upfront: I'm not a traditional developer. I can't be your on-call tech person or commit to ongoing maintenance. What I can do is use AI tools to investigate and fix specific problems. If it needs regular support, you'd want someone proper for that."

If they push:
> "Sure, show me — I'll tell you what I think the issue is. No promises until I see it."

---

## PART 5 — THINGS TO AVOID SAYING

**Don't say:**
- "We're the same as you but smaller" — you're not the same. Different product.
- "We're Malaysia's #1 anything" — too early, sounds desperate
- "We'd love your help to grow" — makes you sound like you need them. You don't.
- "We can promote each other" — vague. Always follow with something specific.
- "Let me know if you want to collab" — puts the work on her. Come with a specific idea.
- "We're just starting out so..." — don't apologise for being new.

**Do say:**
- Specific numbers when you have them (60+ tournaments, RM1M+ prizes, 112 followers, saves metric)
- "We complement each other" not "we compete"
- "Let's try something small first and see how it lands"
- "What would be most useful for your audience?"

---

## PART 6 — HOW TO CLOSE THE MEETING

If it's going well and you want to move forward on something specific:

> "I think the cleanest first step is the editorial-to-registration thing. You publish a piece on an upcoming tournament, we're the link at the bottom for registration. Low effort, we see if the audience responds. The site is already live so we can do this any time. What do you think?"

If she's interested but vague:

> "Let's not over-plan it. How about we both go away, think about what would actually be useful, and reconnect in a couple of weeks? I can share tracker access so you can see what the data looks like."

If she's clearly just exploring and nothing specific is emerging:

> "This was genuinely useful just to understand what you're building. Let's stay in touch — I'll message you when we drop the website launch post. Would be good to have you see it."

**Always leave with a specific next step.** Even if it's just "I'll message you this week."

---

## PART 7 — THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

The meeting is not a pitch. It's a conversation between two people building things in the same space.

Ask more than you tell. Listen for what they actually need, not what you assume they need. The best collaborations happen because both sides felt understood — not because one side had a perfect deck.

If you walk out of there and Grace thinks *"that person is building something real and they know exactly what they're doing"* — the meeting was a success, regardless of whether anything was agreed.

The deal comes later. The relationship is what you're building today.
